# Spoken Delivery Guide

Use this reference when converting written text into natural spoken language for teleprompter delivery. It is a guide for internal reasoning, not content to summarize for the user.

## Core Principle

Written language is designed to be scanned, reread, and visually structured. Spoken language is heard once, in real time. Convert formal, dense, visually structured text into clear, rhythmic, connected speech.

## Patterns To Adapt

### Complex Syntax

Break layered subordinate clauses into simpler sentences. Move the main point earlier.

Written: "Although the weather was unfavorable, the event proceeded as planned."

Spoken: "The weather was bad, but the event still happened."

Written: "Despite the challenges, the project was ultimately successful."

Spoken: "The project was successful, even though there were challenges."

### Elevated Vocabulary

Replace formal or bureaucratic wording with everyday speech.

Prefer "use" over "utilize", "so" over "therefore", and "but" over "notwithstanding".

Written: "The KPI variance triggered a strategic reallocation of resources."

Spoken: "Because of the numbers, we had to shift resources."

### Elliptical Structures

Expand fragments that rely on reader inference.

Written: "Further research required."

Spoken: "I think we will need to do more research on this."

Written: "If necessary, adjustments will be made."

Spoken: "We will make changes if we need to."

### Structured Formatting

Do not keep bullet points or numbered lists in the final teleprompter script. Convert lists into full, flowing sentences with clear transitions.

Written list:

- Improve service
- Cut costs
- Expand markets

Spoken: "We are focused on three things: improving service, cutting costs, and expanding into new markets."

Write the final script in paragraph-style, continuous text only.

### Figurative And Literary Language

Tone down metaphors, symbolism, wordplay, alliteration, or theatrical phrasing when they would sound forced aloud.

Written: "The policy is the lighthouse guiding us through stormy seas."

Spoken: "This policy is helping us stay focused during a tough time."

### Punctuation-Dependent Meaning

Written text often relies on dashes, parentheses, colons, semicolons, bullets, or numbered lists. These can be hard to interpret in real-time delivery.

Avoid embedded asides in dashes or parentheses. Convert them into full spoken sentences.

Written: "The rollout, though delayed, was a success."

Spoken: "The rollout was a success, even though it was delayed."

Rephrase punctuation-heavy constructions.

Written: "We hit our goals: revenue grew, costs dropped, and morale improved."

Spoken: "We hit our goals. Revenue grew. Costs dropped. And morale improved."

For final scripts:

- Do not use bullet points.
- Do not use numbered lists.
- Do not use dash-separated ideas.
- Use full sentences and paragraph-style continuous text.
- If a list is needed, incorporate it into spoken phrasing such as "first", "next", or "we are focusing on".

### Passive Voice And Nominalization

Prefer active voice and verbs over abstract nouns.

Written: "The decision was made by the leadership team."

Spoken: "The leadership team made the decision."

Written: "We conducted an analysis of the data."

Spoken: "We analysed the data."

### Cohesion Markers And Logical Connectors

Replace stiff connectors with natural ones.

Written: "However, we must remain vigilant."

Spoken: "But we still need to stay sharp."

Use everyday transitions such as "so", "but", "and", "also", "now", "let us move on", and "here is why that matters".

### Impersonal Or Institutional Tone

Make institutional writing feel direct when appropriate.

Written: "The organization has decided to restructure the department."

Spoken: "We have decided to restructure the department."

Use "we", "you", and direct address when the source permits it and it serves the speaker-audience relationship.

### Information-Dense Sentences

Split sentences that contain several ideas.

Written: "The system upgrade, implemented over the weekend, included security patches, interface redesigns, and new analytics features."

Spoken: "We upgraded the system over the weekend. It now has new security patches, a redesigned interface, and better analytics."

Replace condensed phrases such as "in light of recent developments" or "subsequent to evaluation" with clear speech such as "because of what happened" or "after we reviewed everything".

### Rhythm And Breath

Add natural rhythm with short sentences, paragraph breaks, contrast, rhetorical questions, and simple emphasis. Make the script easy to breathe through and easy to read aloud.

Avoid visual-only formatting as the main signal of emphasis.

### Visual Reliance

Replace references to layout, links, footnotes, tables, charts, or appendices with spoken guidance.

Written: "For more info, see the appendix or visit the intranet page."

Spoken: "If you want more details, check out the intranet. We have posted the full guide there."

If a chart or visual must be referenced, guide the viewer verbally.

### Formatting-Based Emphasis

Bold, italics, underlines, and headings do not carry into speech. Convert emphasis into sentence structure, repetition, or plain language.

Written emphasis: "This is important."

Spoken emphasis: "This part is really important."

Convert headings into spoken signposts such as "Here is the key takeaway" or "Let us move to the second part."

### Lack Of Redundancy

Listeners cannot reread. Add light reinforcement, signposting, and repetition where needed.

Spoken example: "Here is what matters: trust. Trust in the team, trust in the plan, and trust in the process."

Use cues such as "Let me explain", "Here is why that matters", and "Before we finish".

### Uniform Tone Or Register

Adjust energy to fit the moment. Informative, serious, celebratory, or motivational passages should not all sound the same.

Spoken example: "This part was tough, no doubt about it. But here is the good news."

Match the speaker's likely delivery style and the emotional intent of the message.

## Final Checklist

Before returning the script, check for:

- Complex syntax
- Elevated vocabulary
- Elliptical fragments
- Structured formatting
- Forced figurative language
- Punctuation-dependent meaning
- Passive voice
- Nominalization
- Stiff connectors
- Impersonal tone
- Information-dense sentences
- Missing rhythm or breath cues
- Visual or layout reliance
- Formatting-based emphasis
- Insufficient signposting or reinforcement
- Flat or mismatched tone
